Industries make the switch to carbon credits

Developed countries have thought of a way to cut back on the ill effects of industrial carbon pollutants, which cause green house gases. They buy carbon credits from developing nations to offset their carbon emissions.

Measures by the Kyoto Protocol were created to encourage industries in developing countries to adhere to specific standards. These standards under quotas, provide an assigned amount units (AAUs) credits to emit one metric tonne of carbon dioxide equivalent. The utilisation of carbon credits are monitored and validated by the United Nations Framework Convention of Climate Change (UNFCCC). Industries that have not utilised all their carbon credits sell their carbon credits and businesses that exceed the quota, buy extra allowances on credit.

India and China are the largest sellers of carbon credits and India contributes $1 billion out of the total of $5 billion. On the other hand, Europe is the largest buyer of carbon credits. The Union Ministry of Environment, Forests and Climate Change (MoEF&CC) has set stricter standards for coal-based thermal power plants in India. The standards include a reduction in emissions of particulate matter by 25 per cent, mercury by 75 per cent, sulphur dioxide by 90 per cent and nitrogen oxide by 70 per cent and cut water wastage. This will ensure that more industries follow suit in accordance with the Paris agreement of 2015.

One hundred and fourteen projects were registered in India, making it the highest for any country. In 2011, Reliance Power gained the approval of carbon credits at an estimated Rs.20 billion over a 10 year period. Recently, the United Nations certified the Delhi Metro Rail Corporation as the only rail-based and metro rail in the entire globe to gain from carbon credits by reducing the greenhouse gas emissions by 6.3 lakh tons since inception.

Under the National Clean Development Mechanism Authority (NCDMA), 2195 projects have been approved. The NCDMA has estimated a revenue generation though the sale of carbon credits to a tune of over Rs.10,000 crores. The Tata Group, Reliance, Bajaj, Gujarat Fluorochemicals Limited, ITC and Adani are some of largest Indian corporations that are beneficiaries of CDM. The Paris agreement has passed a resolution to create a carbon market to regulate the buying and selling of carbon credits. With the Union Ministry of Environment, Forests and Climate Change enforcing new standards, more industries will register with NCDMA and UNFCCC to benefit from carbon credits and create less polluting and greener industries.

PlantBottle damage the environment

The PlantBottle, introduced in 2010, is a one of a kind PET bottle manufactured by Coca-Cola, in which the company states that is fully recyclable and made partially from sugarcane. Three main arguments state that Coca-Cola reduces their dependency on fossil fuels and improves the chances of the bottle being recyclable. The issue is that these bottles cannot be recycled as bioplastics are not reusable. Bioplastics contain polylactic acid that is made from corn starch, tapioca, or sugar cane. This form of plastic is removed from regular plastic as recycling centres and treated as waste. The regular PET and HDPE manufacturers produce the right level of flexibility and clarity and the plant- based versions may also contain them. These bottles are made identical to the chemically made bottles in spite of the bottles containing renewable and lower-carbon sources.

How Hoskote became water independent

The farmers in Kuruballahalli, Hoskote were faced with a dilemma. After several years of heavy utilisation of water due to irrigating cash crops like roses, the water table had depleted to levels past 1000 metres. It was ignorance of how that water got there in the first place. They thought it was a never ending supply.

Roses need a lot of water to grow and since electricity is given free to the farmers, they left their pumps running exploiting and depleting the ground water table, letting the water be pumped out for as long as there was electricity.

These days farmers have to spend several lakhs to find newer and newer and deeper bore wells. That was when the government and the World Bank stepped in and began to educate them on harvesting the rain on their land. The farmers were trained to dig trenches on the land, to hold back the run- off and let it percolate into the soil.

Slowly, the government helped fund and built check dams and began to line the walls of the drains to hold the run off for the rain water. After a heavy downpour, the rain water would back up against the check dam and slowly percolate into the soil and replenish the entire ground water table. A small pond was dug and water collected in it from different areas in the village. JCB's have been pressed into use to dig out large ponds in the village by the government and the ponds are being lined with thick plastic sheets so the water is saved to be used in particular fields. The efficacy of saving all rain water to help themselves has become a rule in this village in Hoskote.

“For the last five years we have been buying tankers of water and we can't afford it,” said Narsimappa, a farmer in the village. After a meeting with the Chairman, he understood that it was the harvested rain water to irrigate his land. He added, “On my land I blocked all the run off with trenches and when it rained we ran out to check that no water could leave our land." The village got together and started using an old and defunct stone quarry to store water and reap the benefits of pure and pristine rain water for their crops. The Thasildar of the village maintains rain water charts everyday and continues to press forward with other methods to save water and reduce the excess seepage into the soil.

Mango trees flowering in December?

Bangaloreans have been subject to the extremes of weather with dry monsoons, hot summers and winters that have set in late. This creates unusual temperatures for the mango tree that flowers normally in late March/ April to bring us fruit in June and after.

Over the past few years, across Bangalore, mango trees have been flowering from November. This creates an offset in the cycle as the trees bear fruit at odd intervals, which were previously unheard of. The blooms are not just a small streak of flowers but the entire tree is encompassed with flowers. Rajiv Mathias, Photographer and Former Alumni of St. Joseph's College explains, “It is exciting yet sad for the trees as we now get to enjoy mangoes at unseasonal times. The reason for this is based on the odd climate that these trees are facing.”

In Karnataka, mango trees yield 1,130 thousand metric tons of mangoes and makes the states the fifth largest producer of mangoes in the country. The crop accounts for nearly 23% of all fruit produced in India. The largest mango growing belts in Karnataka are Kolar, Bangalore, Tumkur and Kagu. The Mallika, Dashahari, Alfanzo, Chausa and Himsagar are the some of the cash crops of states like Bihar and account for good results.

According to the National Department of Agriculture, South Africa, the Mango crop can be harvested in most warm climatic conditions, ranging from very hot, very humid to cool and dry, to very hot and arid and can survive in temperatures of 45 oC. The mango trees require to flower for a minimum of two months before the trees bear fruit.

Maharastra had recorded that 80% of the state's crop was ruined due to climate change. In 2011, the state produced only 7,000 boxes of mangoes instead of 70,000 boxes that year due to unseasonal weather patterns.

A.N. Yellappa Reddy, the Former Secretary, Department of Ecology and Environment says, “The trees are subject to a rise in temperature and due to the amount of smoke of the vehicular traffic. Temperature is the main element that modulates whether the mango tree would flower and eventually fruit.”

Though these unseasonal flowering of the mango trees remains scientifically unproven, there remains a sense of certainty that Climate Change is a key driving factor in creating this vicious cycle, which is sad for mango lovers across the world.

Which plants are natural pest controllers

Pests have been associated with plants from time immemorial. It was only in the early 1940s that the farmers in the USA adopted chemical pesticides to treat their crops to be able to yield a larger harvest. In the recent years, pesticides have come with numerous hazards that destroyed the fertility of the soil and more importantly, caused severe health issues with those in direct contact with it.

In India, there are several plants that have the ability to repel insects and other pests. Many agriculturists sow the seeds of these plants in order to protect their crops and these crops are known as Companion plants.

Agriculturist choose Companion plants because they provide a hedged investment, increase yield, create a protective shelter, increase pest suppression by preventing pest and fungi from destroying the crop and used as trap cropping agents. Trap cropping allows for specific sets of plants to attract insects and other pests away from the crops that are later harvested.

Farmers have also taken to beneficial insects which prey on harmful insects and larvae. These include the brachonids, chalcids, ichneumon wasps, ladybugs, lacewings, hover-flies, praying mantis and nematodes. These insects can be attracted to fields and gardens and are natural predators to several pests and in the process stops farmers from spraying their crops with harmful pesticides. Look no further than the humble Mint or Citronella Grass to weed out those uninvited guests. Coriander repels aphids, colorado potato beetles and spider mites. Coriander can be found in super markets and as a companion plant, it can supplement as a crop that you can enjoy along with corresponding crop that you are harvesting.

The Common Lantana was first introduced in the Himalayan region in 1807 and has now spread to almost all parts of India. It was brought into the Indian subcontinent particularly due to its delicate flowers but is now used significantly as an agricultural method to ward off pests and insecticides. The pretty pink Geraniums have over 442 species and are known to repeal leafhoppers, corn earworms and the Small White. Garlic is a natural pest deterrent and is known to chase away rabbits as well. Other important pest controlling plants are the onion, oregano, parsley, peppermint, radish, tobacco and the tomato.

Through the use of companion plants, farmers and gardeners can now protect themselves from harmful insecticides and pesticides, and not have to set aside a portion of the crop which had gone bad due to unchecked use of these sprays. In turn, these plants that are natural pest controllers can be enjoyed along with the harvest and supplement the final produce.
